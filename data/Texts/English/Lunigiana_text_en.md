## Lunigiana

### Introduction

In 1306, Dante Alighieri stayed in the Lunigiana, welcomed by the powerful _Malaspina_ family, rulers of the territory. In particular, he was hosted by _Franceschino Malaspina_ in _Mulazzo_, the historic capital of the “_Spino Secco_” branch.  
During this stay, Dante played an important political and diplomatic role: he acted as a peace mediator in the dispute between the _Malaspina_ and the bishop of Luni, which ended with the famous Peace of Castelnuovo on October 6, 1306.  
Dante’s connection to the Lunigiana is clearly reflected in the *Divina Commedia*, where he demonstrates a detailed knowledge of the territory and its rulers, particularly the _Malaspina_.  
Some aspects of his stay remain debated, especially the exact duration and the precise locations Dante visited within the Lunigiana.

#### Places associated with Dante

- _Museo Dantesco Lunigianese_  
- _Castello di Giovagallo_

### Museo Dantesco Lunigianese

The _Museo Dantesco Lunigianese_ is located in the historic center of _Mulazzo_ and is housed in a 13th-century tower house within the medieval town walls.  
Founded in 2003 by the _Centro Lunigianese di Studi Danteschi_, the museum is dedicated to Dante’s relationship with the Lunigiana and collects documents, educational panels, and materials related to the poet’s presence in the area.  
The exhibition focuses especially on Dante’s 1306 stay and the references to Lunigiana in his work.  
The association between this specific building and Dante’s actual residence is symbolic: there is no evidence that the poet actually lived in this tower house.

### Castello di Giovagallo

The _Castello di Giovagallo_, now in ruins, was one of the residences of the _Malaspina_ and is located near _Mulazzo_.  
Here, Dante met _Moroello Malaspina_, marquis of _Giovagallo_, an important figure in his Lunigiana experience.  
The poet recalls him indirectly in the *Divina Commedia* through a vivid image connected to the territory:


_"I of that city was which to the Baptist_  
_Changed its first patron, wherefore he for this_ <br><br> 
_Forever with his art will make it sad._ 
_And were it not that on the pass of Arno_  
_Some glimpses of him are remaining still,_  <br><br>
_Those citizens, who afterwards rebuilt it_
_Upon the ashes left by Attila,_  
_In vain had caused their labor to be done._<br><br>
_Of my own house I made myself a gibbet."_  
Divina Commedia, Inferno, XIII
