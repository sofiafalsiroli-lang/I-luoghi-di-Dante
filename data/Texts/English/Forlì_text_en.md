## Forlì

### Introduction

After his exile from Florence in 1302, Dante Alighieri found refuge in several cities of central and northern Italy, including Forlì, an important Ghibelline center in Romagna.  
His presence in the city took place in the early years of exile, within the political network of the White Guelphs. Dante was hosted in 1303 by _Scarpetta Ordelaffi_, who welcomed many exiled Florentines.

#### Places associated with Dante

- _Porta Schiavonia_  
- _Palazzo Paulucci di Calboli d’Aste_  
- _Piazza Saffi_

### Porta Schiavonia

_Porta Schiavonia_ was one of the main entrances to the medieval city for travelers coming from Tuscany, along the Montone Valley. Even today, it symbolizes the gateway to Forlì along the Dante itinerary.  
On the facade of a nearby building, a plaque displays a quotation from the _Commedia_ related to the Montone River:

_"Even as that stream which holdeth its own course_  
_The first from Monte Veso tow'rds the East,_  
_Upon the left-hand slope of Apennine,_<br><br> 
_Which is above called Acquacheta, ere_  
_It down descendeth into its low bed,_  
_And at Forlì is vacant of that name,"_   Divina Commedia, Inferno, XVI, 94-99 


### Palazzo Paulucci di Calboli d’Aste

The _Palazzo Paulucci di Calboli d’Aste_, built in its current form in the 18th century, belongs to the _Calboli_ family, one of the most important in Forlì’s history.  
Dante also remembers the family through _Rinieri de’ Calboli_, an illustrious ancestor, as an example of civic virtue.  
On the building’s facade, a plaque displays Dante’s verses dedicated to the family and the Romagna territory:


_"This is Renier; this is the boast and honour_  
_Of the house of Calboli, where no one since_  
_Has made himself the heir of his desert._  
Divina Commedia, Purgatorio, XIV, 88-96 



### Piazza Saffi

_Piazza Saffi_ represents the political and civil center of medieval Forlì.  
Important historical events took place here, including the so-called “_sanguinoso mucchio_” of 1282, an episode linked to the resistance against the French led by the Papacy.  
At that time, the city was at the heart of tensions between the Guelph and Ghibelline factions and the Papacy, which supported French intervention.  
The citizens of Forlì courageously opposed the French troops, resulting in bloody clashes in the square, from which the episode takes its name.  
Dante references this event in the *Inferno*:

_"The city which once made the long resistance,_  
_And of the French a sanguinary heap,_  
_Beneath the Green Paws finds itself again;"_  Divina Commedia, Inferno, XXVII, 43-45
