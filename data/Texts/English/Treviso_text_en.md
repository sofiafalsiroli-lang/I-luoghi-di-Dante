## Treviso

### Introduction

During his exile, Dante Alighieri had contacts with the Veneto area and likely with _Treviso_, a city linked to the _da Camino_ family, an important local lordship.  
While Dante’s connection to the city is primarily literary, a much more concrete link is with his son _Pietro Alighieri_, who lived in _Treviso_, served as its podestà, and died there in 1364.

#### Places associated with Dante

- _Ponte Dante_
- _Chiesa di San Francesco_

### Ponte Dante

The _Ponte Dante_ is located where the _Cagnan_ River flows into the _Sile_, one of the most picturesque spots in the city.  
Originally known as the “bridge of the impossible” due to construction difficulties, it was renamed in 1865 for the sixth centenary of Dante’s birth.  
On that occasion, a commemorative stele was erected featuring the poet’s profile and a famous verse from the *Paradiso*, making the site one of the main Dantean memory points in the city:


 _"And where the Sile and Cagnano join"_   
Divina Commedia, Paradiso, IX, 49 

### Chiesa di San Francesco

The _Chiesa di San Francesco_, built in the 13th century, is one of _Treviso_’s main religious buildings and preserves significant artistic and historical heritage.  
Inside, it houses the tomb of _Pietro Alighieri_, Dante’s son, who lived extensively in the Veneto and died in _Treviso_ in 1364.  
The connection to Dante is therefore indirect: there is no evidence that the poet himself frequented the church, but the presence of his son makes the site particularly significant in Dantean memory.
