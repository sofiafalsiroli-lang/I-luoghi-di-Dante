## Verona

### Introduction

In 1303, shortly after his exile from Florence, Dante Alighieri found one of his first refuges in _Verona_ at the court of _Bartolomeo della Scala_, lord of the city from 1301 to 1304 and a member of the powerful _Scaliger_ family.  
In a Ghibelline-oriented _Verona_, open to hosting political exiles and intellectuals, Dante was welcomed with the courtesy and generosity traditionally attributed to the “_gran Lombardo_” mentioned in the *Paradiso*:  

_"Thine earliest refuge and thine earliest inn_  
_Shall be the mighty Lombard's courtesy,_  
_Who on the Ladder bears the holy bird,_    
Divina Commedia, Paradiso, XVII, 70-72 

After this first stay, Dante returned to _Verona_ between 1312 and 1318, finding protection under _Cangrande della Scala_, a central figure in Italian politics of the time and a well-known patron.  
During this longer and more stable period, the city became not only a refuge for the poet but also a lively cultural center where he could continue his literary and political activities, strengthening the connection between his exile experience and the _Scaliger_ environment.

#### Places associated with Dante

- _Piazza Dante_
- _Chiesa di Sant'Elena_
- _Casa di Romeo_
- _Porta Borsari_

### Piazza dei Signori, also called Piazza Dante

_Piazza dei Signori_, also known as _Piazza Dante_, located in the heart of _Verona_, hosts a statue of the poet and symbolizes the local memory of Dante’s stay in the Scaliger city.  
The modern statue depicts Dante in a thoughtful pose, gazing toward the city center, commemorating not only his presence but also the significance of his work for Veronese and Italian culture.

### Chiesa di Sant'Elena

The _Chiesa di Sant’Elena_ in _Verona_ is one of the main religious sites in the historic center.  
Local tradition closely associates it with Dante’s stay at the court of _Cangrande della Scala_.  
According to chronicles, in January 1320, Dante presented to the canons and local scholars his famous _"Quaestio de aqua et terra"_, a Latin treatise discussing one of the most debated cosmological questions of the time: whether water naturally lies above or below the land relative to sea level.

### Casa di Romeo

The so-called _Casa di Romeo_ in _Verona_, located in the historic center, is a symbolic site linked to the city’s Shakespearean legend.  
It also holds particular significance in the Dantean context. In the *Purgatorio*, Dante invites the emperor to observe the tragic political situation in Italy, marked by rivalry and internal conflicts, citing the families of the _Montecchi_ and the _Cappelletti_ as examples, who would later appear in Shakespeare’s famous drama:

_"Come and behold Montecchi and Cappelletti,_  
_Monaldi and Fillippeschi, careless man!_
_Those sad already, and these doubt-depressed!"_
Divina Commedia, Purgatorio, VI, 106-108 

### Porta Borsari

_Porta Borsari_, one of the main gates of Roman and medieval _Verona_, marked the boundary between the city and the roads connecting to the Po Valley.  
Located along the historic _Via Postumia_, it was a strategic passage for merchants, pilgrims, and travelers, as well as a symbolic place of urban life.  
A plaque on this stretch of the city commemorates the _Palio del Drappo Verde_, an ancient medieval race. Tradition holds that teams from different districts competed along the main streets, and Dante refers to this race in the *Divina Commedia* as a symbol to describe movement, competitions, and social rivalries:


_"Then he turned round, and seemed to be of those_  
_Who at Verona run for the Green Mantle_  
_Across the plain; and seemed to be among them_<br><br>
_The one who wins, and not the one who loses._  
Divina Commedia, Inferno, XV, vv. 121‑123
