Forlì

Introduction

After his exile from Florence in 1302, Dante Alighieri found refuge in several cities of central and northern Italy, including Forlì, an important Ghibelline center in Romagna.
His presence in the city took place in the early years of exile, within the political network of the White Guelphs. Dante was hosted in 1303 by Scarpetta Ordelaffi, who welcomed many exiled Florentines.

Porta Schiavonia

Porta Schiavonia was one of the main entrances to the medieval city for travelers coming from Tuscany, along the Montone Valley. Even today, it symbolizes the gateway to Forlì along the Dante itinerary.
On the facade of a nearby building, a plaque displays a quotation from the Commedia related to the Montone River:

"Even as that stream which holdeth its own course
The first from Monte Veso tow'rds the East,
Upon the left-hand slope of Apennine,

Which is above called Acquacheta, ere
It down descendeth into its low bed,
And at Forlì is vacant of that name,"
Divina Commedia, Inferno, XVI, 94-99

Palazzo Paulucci di Calboli D'Aste

The Palazzo Paulucci di Calboli d’Aste, built in its current form in the 18th century, belongs to the Calboli family, one of the most important in Forlì’s history.
Dante also remembers the family through Rinieri de’ Calboli, an illustrious ancestor, as an example of civic virtue.
On the building’s facade, a plaque displays Dante’s verses dedicated to the family and the Romagna territory:

"This is Renier; this is the boast and honour
Of the house of Calboli, where no one since
Has made himself the heir of his desert.
Divina Commedia, Purgatorio, XIV, 88-96

Piazza Saffi

Piazza Saffi represents the political and civil center of medieval Forlì.
Important historical events took place here, including the so-called “sanguinoso mucchio” of 1282, an episode linked to the resistance against the French led by the Papacy.
At that time, the city was at the heart of tensions between the Guelph and Ghibelline factions and the Papacy, which supported French intervention.
The citizens of Forlì courageously opposed the French troops, resulting in bloody clashes in the square, from which the episode takes its name.
Dante references this event in the Inferno:

"The city which once made the long resistance,
And of the French a sanguinary heap,
Beneath the Green Paws finds itself again;"
Divina Commedia, Inferno, XXVII, 43-45
