Bologna

Introduction

During his exile, Dante Alighieri also spent time in Bologna, one of the most influential cities of medieval Italy, renowned for its university and vibrant cultural and legal life. Details about Dante’s stay in Bologna remain uncertain: historians cannot pinpoint exactly when or for how long he resided there. However, it is widely accepted that he frequented the city between the late 13th and early 14th centuries, likely to study and engage in cultural exchanges. Bologna plays a notable role in Dante’s work, not so much for documented biographical events, but for the numerous references in the Divina Commedia, which reveal his intimate knowledge of the city and its political and social environment.

Torre della Garisenda

Built in the 12th century and standing about 48 meters tall today, the Torre della Garisenda is one of Bologna’s iconic “Two Towers.” Its distinctive tilt, caused by structural problems already present in medieval times, made it an unmistakable landmark in the city skyline. Dante explicitly references it in the Divina Commedia, using the tower as a visual comparison for the giant Antaeus:

"As seems the Carisenda, to behold
Beneath the leaning side, when goes a cloud
Above it so that opposite it hangs;

Such did Antæus seem to me, who stood
Watching to see him stoop, and then it was
I could have wished to go some other way."
Divina Commedia, Inferno, XXXI, 136-138

Basilica of San Domenico

Basilica di San Domenico Basilica di San Domenico
Photo: Elemari-r95, CC BY-SA 4.0, via Wikimedia Commons

The Basilica di San Domenico is one of the main religious landmarks in Bologna, built starting in the 13th century and closely associated with Domenico di Guzmán, founder of the Dominican Order, who is buried here. Inside the basilica, a plaque displays verses from the Paradiso dedicated to the saint:

"Dominic was he called; and him I speak of
Even as of the husbandman whom Christ
Elected to his garden to assist him."
Divina Commedia, Paradiso, XII, 70-72

These lines appear in the sphere of the Sun, where Dante encounters the wise spirits, including San Bonaventura, who recounts the life of San Domenico.

Former Convent of the Frati Gaudenti

At number 40 Via Arienti, a plaque marks the site of the former convent of the so-called Frati Gaudenti, members of the Order of the Knights of the Blessed Virgin Mary Glorious, founded in the 13th century in Bologna to maintain civil peace. Dante places them among the hypocrites in the Inferno, criticizing their moral corruption:

"Frati Gaudenti were we, and Bolognese;
I Catalano, and he Loderingo
Named, and together taken by thy city,
As the wont is to take one man alone,

For maintenance of its peace; and we were such
That still it is apparent round Gardingo."
Divina Commedia, Inferno, XXIII, 103-108
