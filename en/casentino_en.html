Casentino

Introduction

During his exile, Dante Alighieri found refuge in the Casentino, a valley in the Tuscan Apennines, hosted by the noble Guidi family, who controlled the area and managed numerous castles and villages.
It is likely that Dante stayed in several locations across the Casentino, moving between different castles and residences of the Guidi family.
In this territory, Dante engaged in political mediation, interacting with local lords and navigating tensions between Guelphs and Ghibellines—experiences that would later influence some of his political reflections in the Divina Commedia.

Castello di Poppi

The Castello di Poppi, built in the 13th century by the Conti Guidi, dominates the village and its main square.
Dante probably stayed here during his exile, as suggested by local sources and historical chronicles.
The village and the castle represent a typical example of a medieval fortified center, serving both defensive and administrative purposes.

Castello di Romena

The Castello di Romena, built in the 12th century by the Conti Guidi, overlooks the village and the Casentino valley. According to tradition, Dante Alighieri stayed here during his exile.
The territory is also associated with the character Mastro Adamo, whom Dante places in the Inferno, where he is punished as a coin forger.
