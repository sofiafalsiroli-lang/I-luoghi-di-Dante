Lunigiana

Introduction

In 1306, Dante Alighieri stayed in the Lunigiana, welcomed by the powerful Malaspina family, rulers of the territory. In particular, he was hosted by Franceschino Malaspina in Mulazzo, the historic capital of the “Spino Secco” branch.
During this stay, Dante played an important political and diplomatic role: he acted as a peace mediator in the dispute between the Malaspina and the bishop of Luni, which ended with the famous Peace of Castelnuovo on October 6, 1306.
Dante’s connection to the Lunigiana is clearly reflected in the Divina Commedia, where he demonstrates a detailed knowledge of the territory and its rulers, particularly the Malaspina.
Some aspects of his stay remain debated, especially the exact duration and the precise locations Dante visited within the Lunigiana.

Museo Dantesco Lunigianese

The Museo Dantesco Lunigianese is located in the historic center of Mulazzo and is housed in a 13th-century tower house within the medieval town walls.
Founded in 2003 by the Centro Lunigianese di Studi Danteschi, the museum is dedicated to Dante’s relationship with the Lunigiana and collects documents, educational panels, and materials related to the poet’s presence in the area.
The exhibition focuses especially on Dante’s 1306 stay and the references to Lunigiana in his work.
The association between this specific building and Dante’s actual residence is symbolic: there is no evidence that the poet actually lived in this tower house.

Castello di Giovagallo

The Castello di Giovagallo, now in ruins, was one of the residences of the Malaspina and is located near Mulazzo.
Here, Dante met Moroello Malaspina, marquis of Giovagallo, an important figure in his Lunigiana experience.
The poet recalls him indirectly in the Divina Commedia through a vivid image connected to the territory:

"I of that city was which to the Baptist
Changed its first patron, wherefore he for this

Forever with his art will make it sad.
And were it not that on the pass of Arno
Some glimpses of him are remaining still,

Those citizens, who afterwards rebuilt it
Upon the ashes left by Attila,
In vain had caused their labor to be done.

Of my own house I made myself a gibbet."
Divina Commedia, Inferno, XIII
