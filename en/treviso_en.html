Treviso

Introduction

During his exile, Dante Alighieri had contacts with the Veneto area and likely with Treviso, a city linked to the da Camino family, an important local lordship.
While Dante’s connection to the city is primarily literary, a much more concrete link is with his son Pietro Alighieri, who lived in Treviso, served as its podestà, and died there in 1364.

Ponte Dante

The Ponte Dante is located where the Cagnan River flows into the Sile, one of the most picturesque spots in the city.
Originally known as the “bridge of the impossible” due to construction difficulties, it was renamed in 1865 for the sixth centenary of Dante’s birth.
On that occasion, a commemorative stele was erected featuring the poet’s profile and a famous verse from the Paradiso, making the site one of the main Dantean memory points in the city:

"And where the Sile and Cagnano join"
Divina Commedia, Paradiso, IX, 49

Chiesa di San Francesco

The Chiesa di San Francesco, built in the 13th century, is one of Treviso’s main religious buildings and preserves significant artistic and historical heritage.
Inside, it houses the tomb of Pietro Alighieri, Dante’s son, who lived extensively in the Veneto and died in Treviso in 1364.
The connection to Dante is therefore indirect: there is no evidence that the poet himself frequented the church, but the presence of his son makes the site particularly significant in Dantean memory.
