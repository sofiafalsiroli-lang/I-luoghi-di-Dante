Verona

Introduction

In 1303, shortly after his exile from Florence, Dante Alighieri found one of his first refuges in Verona at the court of Bartolomeo della Scala, lord of the city from 1301 to 1304 and a member of the powerful Scaliger family.
In a Ghibelline-oriented Verona, open to hosting political exiles and intellectuals, Dante was welcomed with the courtesy and generosity traditionally attributed to the “gran Lombardo” mentioned in the Paradiso:

"Thine earliest refuge and thine earliest inn
Shall be the mighty Lombard's courtesy,
Who on the Ladder bears the holy bird,"
Divina Commedia, Paradiso, XVII, 70-72

After this first stay, Dante returned to Verona between 1312 and 1318, finding protection under Cangrande della Scala, a central figure in Italian politics of the time and a well-known patron.
During this longer and more stable period, the city became not only a refuge for the poet but also a lively cultural center where he could continue his literary and political activities, strengthening the connection between his exile experience and the Scaliger environment.

Piazza dei Signori (Piazza Dante)

Piazza dei Signori, also known as Piazza Dante, located in the heart of Verona, hosts a statue of the poet and symbolizes the local memory of Dante’s stay in the Scaliger city.
The modern statue depicts Dante in a thoughtful pose, gazing toward the city center, commemorating not only his presence but also the significance of his work for Veronese and Italian culture.

Chiesa di Sant'Elena

The Chiesa di Sant’Elena in Verona is one of the main religious sites in the historic center.
Local tradition closely associates it with Dante’s stay at the court of Cangrande della Scala.
According to chronicles, in January 1320, Dante presented to the canons and local scholars his famous "Quaestio de aqua et terra", a Latin treatise discussing one of the most debated cosmological questions of the time: whether water naturally lies above or below the land relative to sea level.

Casa di Romeo

The so-called Casa di Romeo in Verona, located in the historic center, is a symbolic site linked to the city’s Shakespearean legend.
It also holds particular significance in the Dantean context. In the Purgatorio, Dante invites the emperor to observe the tragic political situation in Italy, marked by rivalry and internal conflicts, citing the families of the Montecchi and the Cappelletti as examples, who would later appear in Shakespeare’s famous drama:

"Come and behold Montecchi and Cappelletti,
Monaldi and Fillippeschi, careless man!
Those sad already, and these doubt-depressed!"
Divina Commedia, Purgatorio, VI, 106-108

Porta Borsari

Porta Borsari, one of the main gates of Roman and medieval Verona, marked the boundary between the city and the roads connecting to the Po Valley.
Located along the historic Via Postumia, it was a strategic passage for merchants, pilgrims, and travelers, as well as a symbolic place of urban life.
A plaque on this stretch of the city commemorates the Palio del Drappo Verde, an ancient medieval race. Tradition holds that teams from different districts competed along the main streets, and Dante refers to this race in the Divina Commedia as a symbol to describe movement, competitions, and social rivalries:

"Then he turned round, and seemed to be of those
Who at Verona run for the Green Mantle
Across the plain; and seemed to be among them

The one who wins, and not the one who loses."
Divina Commedia, Inferno, XV, 121-123
